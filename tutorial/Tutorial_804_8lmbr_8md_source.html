1 Rescoring Procedures {#rescoring}
2 ====================
3 
4 \section lmbr Lattice Minimum Bayes Risk Decoding
5 
6 For a detailed discussion of LMBR, see Chapters 7 and 8 in [\ref BlackwoodPhD].
7 
8 LMBR is a decoding procedure, based on the following:
9 
10 - Evidence space: a lattice (WFSA) containing weighted translations produced by the SMT system.
11  - N-gram posterior distributions, with pathwise posteriors, are extracted from this WFSA.
12 - The hypotheses space: an unweighted lattice (FSA) containing hypotheses to be rescored.
13 
14 The following steps are carried out in LMBR decoding:
15 
16 1. The evidence space is normalised after applying a grammar scale factor (`--alpha=`). Scaling is done by the \ref OpenFst [Push](http://www.openfst.org/twiki/bin/view/FST/PushDoc) towards final states, and setting the final state probability to 1.0.
17 3. N-grams are extracted from the hypothesis space.
18 4. N-gram path-posterior probabilities are computed over the evidence space using a modified Forward procedure (see \ref Blackwood2010)
19 5. Cyclic WFSAs are built to represent posterior probability distribution of each n-gram order and compose with the original hypotheses space. A word insertion penalty (`--wps=`) is also included in the costs of the cyclic WFSAs.
20 6. Risk is computed through a sequence of compositions.
21 7. The result for LMBR decoding is a WFSA; each weighted path represents a hypothesis and its risk.
22 
23 The weighted hypothesis space can be save as a WFSA, or the minimum risk hypothesis can be
24 generated via the \ref OpenFst
25 [ShortestPath](http://www.openfst.org/twiki/bin/view/FST/ShortestPathDoc)
26 operation.
27 
28 The following example applies LMBR decoding to the baseline lattices
29 
30  > lmbr.O2 --config=configs/CF.baseline.lmbr &> log/log.baseline.lmbr
31 
32 The LMBR output hyppthesis file keeps the scale factor, word penalty, and sentence id at the start of the file;
33 the hypothesis follows the colon
34 
35  > cat output/exp.baseline.lmbr/HYPS/0.40_0.02.hyp
36  0.4 0.02 1:1 3 9121 384 6 2756 7 3 4144 6 159312 42 1341 2
37  0.4 0.02 2:1 3 1119 6 3 9121 1711 54 79 6 3 85 7 525 3 13907 17 3 628 5 2
38 
39 LMBR can be optimised by tuning the grammar scale factor and word insertion penalty.
40 Once lattices are loaded into memory and n-grams are extracted (steps 1 - 5), rescoring is fast enough that
41 it is practical and efficient to perform a grid search over a range of
42 parameter values (see config file).
43 
44 Hypotheses are written to different files, with names based on parameter values (e.g. as
45  `--writeonebest=output/exp.baseline.lmbr/HYPS/%%alpha%%_%%wps%%.hyp` ).
46 The best set of values can be selected based on BLEU score, after
47 mapping each integer mapped output back to words, detokenizing, and scoring on against references.
48 
49 LMBR relies on a unigram precision (p) and precision ratio (r) that are computed over a development set,
50 e.g. with verbose logs of a BLEU scorer such as NIST mteval1.
51 The script `$HiFSTROOT/scripts/lmbr/compute-testset-precisions.pl` is included for this purpose.
52 
53 
54 If the option `--preprune=` is specified, the evidence space is pruned prior to computing posterior probabilities (i.e. pruning is done at threshold 7 in this example). If this option is not defined, the full evidence space will be passed through.
