1 Introduction {#mainpage}
2 ========================
3 
4 
5 This tutorial presents various tools and techniques developed in
6 the [Statistical Machine Translation](http://divf.eng.cam.ac.uk/smt) group at
7 the [Cambridge University Engineering Department](http://eng.cam.ac.uk).
8 
9 The tutorial is intended to serve as a guide for the use of the tools, but our research publications contain the
10 best descriptions of the algorithms and modelling techniques described here.
11 The most relevant publications for this tutorial are listed below (\ref Refs). In
12 particular, this tutorial is based on the Russian-English SMT system
13 developed for the WMT 2013 evaluation - we suggest reading the system description [\ref Pino2013] before starting on the tutorial.
14 
15 Our complete publications can be found at <http://divf.eng.cam.ac.uk/smt/Main/SmtPapers>.

343 \subsection wmaps Word Maps and Integer Mapped Files
344 
345 HiFST uses [symbol tables](http://www.openfst.org/twiki/bin/view/FST/FstAdvancedUsage#Symbol_Tables)
346 as provided by \ref OpenFst to map between source and target
347 language text and the integer representation used internally by
348 the decoder. See the \ref OpenFst [Quick Tour](http://www.openfst.org/twiki/bin/view/FST/FstQuickTour) for a
349 discussion of the use of symbol tables.
350 
351 Integer mappings for English and Russian are in the directory wmaps/ :
352 
353  wmaps/wmt13.en.wmap
354  wmaps/wmt13.en.all.wmap (a much larger version of wmaps/wmt13.en.wmap)
355  wmaps/wmt13.ru.wmap
356  wmaps/wmt13.ru.all.wmap (a much larger version of wmaps/wmt13.ru.wmap)
357 
358 Note that HiFST reserves the integers 1 and 2 for the sentence-start and sentence-end symbols. 0 is the OpenFST epsilon symbol.
359 
360 The format of the wordmap files is straightforward, e.g.
361 
362  > head wmaps/wmt13.en.wmap
363  <epsilon> 0
364  <s> 1
365  </s> 2
366  the 3
367  , 4
368  . 5
369  of 6
370  to 7
371  and 8
372  in 9
373 
374

376 
377  RU/RU.set1.idx : integer mapped Russian text
378 
379  > head -2 RU/RU.set1.idx
380  1 20870 2447 5443 50916 78159 3621 2
381  1 1716 20196 95123 154 1049 6778 996 9 239837 7 1799 4 2

383

469 \subsubsection rules Grammar File Formats
470 
471 In the grammar file, each line represents a rule. The rule format is:
472 
473  LHS RHS_SOURCE RHS_TARGET FEA_1 [FEA_2 FEA_3 FEA_4 ...]
474 
475 where
476 
477  LHS = the left hand side of the rule
478  RHS_SOURCE = the source-language part of the right hand side of the rule
479  RHS_TARGET = the target-language part of the right hand side of the rule
480  FEA_i = the i-th component of the feature vector associated with the rule
481 
482 The left hand side of a rule is a non-terminal symbol (in uppercase).
483 The right hand side is a pair of terminal and non-terminal
484 symbol sequences in the source and target languages.
485 
486 \paragraph tgrammars_formats_fea Feature Vectors
487 
488 Scores are assigned to rules as the dot product of a rule-specific feature vector
489 and a weight vector (see the discussion in \ref mert). This
490 computation can be done offline, in which case the feature for every rule in the grammar is a
491 1-dimensional scalar. Alternatively, the decoder can be provided with a weight vector which is applied
492 to the feature vectors while loading the grammar.
493 
494 For example, the grammar `G/rules.shallow.gz` provided in this tutorial the following set of weights was found via LMERT tuning (see \ref mert ):
495 
496  0.697263,0.396540,2.270819,-0.145200,0.038503,29.518480,-3.411896,-3.732196,0.217455,0.041551,0.060136
497 
498 These can be applied to the Shallow-1 grammar, as follows:
499 
500  > WV=0.697263,0.396540,2.270819,-0.145200,0.038503,29.518480,-3.411896,-3.732196,0.217455,0.041551,0.060136
501  > zcat G/rules.shallow.vecfea.gz | scripts/weightgrammar -w=$WV | head -3
502  V 3 4 -2.046860955276
503  V 3 4_3 -1.884009085882
504  V 3 8 1.857985226112
505 
506 and this should agree with the scalar-valued version of the grammar:
507 
508  > zcat G/rules.shallow.gz | head -3
509  V 3 4 -2.046860955276
510  V 3 4_3 -1.884009085882
511  V 3 8 1.857985226112
512 
513 \paragraph tgrammars_formats_nt Non-Terminals
514 
515 In translation, a non-terminal `X` on the right hand side can be
516 rewritten by any rule whose left hand side is `X`. HiFST places
517 no restrictions on the definition of terminal and non-terminal
518 sequences in an SCFG rule; similarly, there are no constraints on how
519 many non-terminal symbols can be used in a rule (i.e. there are no constraints on order of the
520 SCFG). However using a full Hiero grammar can lead to slow
521 translation, and this tutorial discusses several strategies for
522 pruning in translation and for translation grammar pruning.
523 
524 Here is a small sample translation grammar
525

546 
547 This grammar has four non-terminal symbols: `S`, `M`, `V` and `D`, and a 1-dimensional weight. The terminal symbols are integers, corresponding to words in the source and target language word maps (\ref wmaps). The symbol `<dr>`
548 is a special symbol used by HiFST to represent an empty word, to indicate deletion.
549 
550 
551 As an example, for rule
552 
553  V 164_M_60 78_M_8 -0.226464
554 
555 we have
556 
557  LHS = V
558  RHS_SOURCE = 164_M_60
559  RHS_TARGET = 78_M_8
560  WEIGHT_1 = -0.226464
561 
562 With this rule the decoder can rewrite the non-terminal `V` by replacing it by `164_M_60`
563 in the source language and by `78_M_8`
564 in the target language; the rule is applied with
565 a score of -0.226464. Similarly, rule `V 5 6 -1.81729`
566 replaces `V` by the word "5" in the source-language and with the word
567 "6" in the target-language, with a score of -1.81729.
568 
569 Indices on non-terminals indicate alignment within rules with more than one non-terminal. For example, for rule
570 
571  V 164_M2_6_M1 78_M1_8_M2 -0.226464
572 
573 the `M` non-terminals on both language sides are indexed by 1 or 2; that is, `M1` in the source
574 language is linked with `M1` in the target language, and `M2` in the
575 source language is linked with `M2` in the target.
576 Strictly speaking, `M2` is not a non-terminal in the above example: it is the second
577 instance of the non-terminal `M` in the rule.
578 Obviously this is a reordering rule.
579 
580 In general we use different types of rule to distinguish different
581 translation cases and obtain a finer-grained model. In this example, the `S`
582 rules are doing something rather similar to the glue rules used in
583 Hiero-style systems; the `M` rules can be regarded as monotonic
584 translation rules; the `V` rules can be regarded as reordering
585 translation rules and phrasal translation rules; and the `D` rule can be
586 regarded as an explicit operation of word deletion.

588 Note that there is a straight-forward correspondence between the
589 Hiero-style rule notation introduced by [\ref Chiang2007]
590 and the HiFST rule file format (for rules with
591 one-dimensional weights). For example, the HiFST grammar file entries
592 
593  V 164_M2_6_M1 78_M1_8_M2 -0.226464
594  S S_X S_X 0.05768
595 
596 can be written as
597 
598  V -> < 164 M2 6 M1 , 78 M1 8 M2 > / -0.226464
599  S -> < S X , S X > / 0.05768

602 \subsubsection tgrammars_shallow Shallow-N Translation Grammars
603 
604 Shallow grammars [\ref deGispert2010] can be used to control the degree of nesting allowed within an hierarchical grammar.
605 For example, for a Shallow-1 Grammar, variables in a rule can be substituted only by phrases. That is,
606 hierarchical rules can be used only once to generate words; once a hierarchical rule is used, translation relies on glue rules to cover longer source spans.
607 
608 Formally, we use W to denote the set of terminals, and S
609 and X to denote two non-terminals. A simple
610 Hiero-style grammar can be defined to be:
611 
612  S -> X, X
613  S -> S X, S X
614  X -> a, b where a, b \in ({X} union W)^+
615 
616 We can transform this into a Shallow-1 grammar as
617 
618  S -> X, X
619  S -> S X, S X
620  Y -> a, b where a, b \in {W}^+
621  X -> u, v where u, v \in {{Y} union W}^+
622 
623 Here `Y` is introduced to handle phrasal translations. The variables in rule `X -> u, v` can only be substituted with the `Y` rules.
624 
625 In some cases it is desirable to allow more complex movement in
626 translation, such as complex structure movements in Chinese-English
627 translation. For this we can use a generalisation of the simple
628 Shallow-1 grammar, called Shallow-N grammars. These grammars allow hierarchical
629 rules to be applied up to N times.
630 For example, below is the form of a Shallow-2 grammar.
631 
632  S -> X, X
633  S -> S X, S X
634  Y^0 -> a^0, b^0 where a^0, b^0 \in {W}^+
635  Y^1 -> a^1, b^1 where a^1, b^1 \in {{Y^0} union W}^+
636  X -> u, v where u, v \in {{Y^1} union W}^+
637 
638 
639 The Shallow-2 grammar introduces `Y^0` and `Y^1` to handle hierarchical
640 rule application at two levels within a derivation. For more detailed
641 description of Shallow-n grammars, please refer to the HiFST paper [\ref deGispert2010].
